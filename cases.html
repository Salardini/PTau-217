Clinical Applications of the Alzheimer's Disease Risk Calculator

Clinical Case Studies

The following four cases demonstrate the practical application of the Alzheimer's disease risk calculator across diverse clinical scenarios. Each case illustrates how plasma pTau 217/β-Amyloid 1-42 ratio test results, combined with patient demographics and clinical factors, provide actionable risk assessments for personalized care planning.

These examples highlight the tool's utility in differentiating risk levels, guiding clinical decision-making, and facilitating evidence-based discussions with patients and families about diagnostic workup, monitoring strategies, and intervention planning.

Clinical Vignette

Margaret, a 68-year-old retired teacher, was referred to the memory clinic by her primary care physician after expressing concerns about increasing forgetfulness over the past year. She reports occasional difficulty remembering names and sometimes misplacing items, though her daily functioning remains intact. Her husband confirms subtle changes but notes she continues to manage household finances and medications independently. Neuropsychological testing revealed performance within normal limits for age, and MRI showed only mild age-related changes. Given her persistent worry about Alzheimer's disease due to a family history (mother developed dementia in her 80s), plasma biomarker testing was performed to provide objective risk assessment.

Clinical Interpretation

This 68-year-old woman with subjective cognitive decline has an extremely low risk of developing Alzheimer's disease. The negative biomarker test reduces her risk by 97% compared to age-matched peers.

Clinical Recommendations

Clinical Vignette

Robert, a 74-year-old retired engineer, was brought to the clinic by his daughter who noticed he had been struggling with word-finding and occasionally repeating questions during family gatherings. Comprehensive neuropsychological evaluation confirmed mild cognitive impairment, particularly affecting episodic memory and executive function. His Montreal Cognitive Assessment score was 24/30, representing a decline from his estimated premorbid high-average cognitive abilities. APOE genotyping revealed E3/E4 heterozygosity, placing him at increased genetic risk. Robert remains independent in most activities of daily living but requires assistance with complex financial tasks. The family is seeking guidance about prognosis and whether to proceed with amyloid PET imaging, making plasma biomarker testing an attractive intermediate step for risk stratification.

Clinical Recommendations

Clinical Vignette

David, a 62-year-old university professor, self-referred to the memory clinic after noticing increasing difficulty with lecture preparation and remembering student names over the past 18 months. His wife corroborates progressive memory decline and notes he has become more withdrawn from social activities. Neuropsychological testing revealed significant deficits in episodic memory, working memory, and processing speed, consistent with amnestic MCI. Brain MRI showed mild hippocampal atrophy disproportionate for age. APOE genotyping revealed E4/E4 homozygosity, conferring very high genetic risk for Alzheimer's disease. Given his relatively young age, strong family history (father diagnosed with early-onset AD at age 58), and high genetic risk, there is substantial concern for early-onset Alzheimer's disease. Plasma biomarker testing was ordered to assess amyloid pathology and guide urgent treatment decisions.

Clinical Interpretation

This 62-year-old man with MCI, APOE4 homozygosity, and positive biomarkers has very high risk despite younger age. The combination of genetic and biomarker factors indicates likely early-onset Alzheimer's disease.

Clinical Recommendations

Urgent referral to specialized memory center for comprehensive evaluation. Consider amyloid-targeting therapy eligibility. Initiate care planning discussions and family genetic counseling. Monitor closely for progression.

Clinical Vignette

Eleanor, an 82-year-old former librarian, was brought to the clinic by her son due to progressive cognitive decline over three years. She initially presented with memory complaints and word-finding difficulties, which have progressed to significant impairment in activities of daily living. She now requires assistance with medication management, cooking, and financial tasks, and occasionally becomes confused about time and place. Her Mini-Mental State Exam score is 18/30, and she exhibits clear deficits across multiple cognitive domains including memory, language, and executive function. Brain imaging shows moderate cortical atrophy with hippocampal volume loss consistent with neurodegenerative disease. While the clinical presentation strongly suggests Alzheimer's disease, plasma biomarker testing was performed to confirm amyloid pathology and exclude other dementia subtypes, particularly given the family's questions about treatment options and prognosis.
